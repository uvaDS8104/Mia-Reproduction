TI TAXONOMIC EVOLUTION OF NEOGENE PLANKTONIC FORAMINIFERA AND PALEOCEANOGRAPHIC RELATIONS
SO PALEOCEANOGRAPHY
LA English
DT Article
ID antarctic glaciation; isotope stratigraphy; kinetic-model; pacific-ocean; diversity; sediments; depth; biogeography; equatorial; atlantic
AB The taxonomic evolution of Neogene (24 Ma to present day) planktonic foraminiferal species are described using four indices: rate of speciation, rate of extinction, rate of diversification, and rate of turnover. The evolution of Neogene planktonic foraminifera is marked by three principal stages: (1) a diversification stage (24 to 16 Ma, early Miocene), (2) an equilibrium stage (16 to 5 Ma, middle and late Miocene), and (3) a declining stage (5 Ma to Recent, Pliocene and Quaternary). The early Miocene diversification was in response to an increase in polar to equatorial thermal gradients and related subdivision of surface water masses. During the middle and late Miocene, the assemblages remained close to their equilibria, with relatively low diversification rates. The decline in diversity during the Pliocene and Quaternary probably resulted from adverse effects imposed by both large-scale paleoceanographic fluctuations that began about 6 Ma and high-frequency climatic oscillations related to northern hemisphere glaciations and deglaciations that began at about 2.5 Ma. Bursts of evolutionary activity marked by higher rates of turnover coincided with major paleoceanographic changes. Average evolutionary rates appear to be similar for tropical, transitional, and temperate assemblages, although the actual numbers of extinctions and speciations are higher in tropical assemblages because of higher standing diversity. Contemporaneity of turnover peaks in the three climatic regimes indicates global control of paleoceanographic perturbations upon planktonic foraminiferal evolution. The observed evolutionary pattern supports the stationary model of Stenseth and Maynard Smith (1984).

TI THE CRETACEOUS/TERTIARY BOUNDARY EVENT IN THE NORTH PACIFIC: PLANKTONIC FORAMINIFERAL RESULTS FROM DEEP SEA DRILLING PROJECT SITE 577, SHATSKY RISE
SO PALEOCEANOGRAPHY
LA English
DT Article
ID tertiary boundary; evolution; extinctions; spheroids; sediments; leg-86; model; ocean; end
AB A detailed micropaleontologic analysis of sediments from Deep-Sea Drilling Project site 577 from the Shatsky Rise, North Pacific, was undertaken to describe extinction and radiation patterns of planktonic foraminifera in a continuous carbonate sequence spanning the Cretaceous/Tertiary (K/T) boundary. A 15-m section containing the boundary was closely sampled and contained planktonic foraminiferal assemblages characteristic of the late Maastrichtian Abathomphalus mayaroensis zone through the Danian Globorotalia uncinata zone. The Cretaceous/Tertiary boundary was placed at the abrupt disappearance of almost all Maastrichtian planktonic foraminifera. Coincident with these extinctions was the presence of a large number of "microtektitelike" spherules. The most important faunal change in the late Maastrichtian was an abrupt increase in the relative abundance of the high-latitude species Hedbergella monmouthensis, together with the appearance of diminutive populations of Guembelitria cretacea and Globigerina eugubina approximately 20,000 years before the boundary. These changes are interpreted as indicating a cooling of surface water conditions. Globigerina eugubina and G. cretacea were the only planktonic foraminiferal species to survive the Cretaceous/Tertiary boundary event at this locality. The delta O-18 and delta C-13 values of G. eugubina from below the K/T boundary are nearly the same as those from above the boundary, but in both cases the delta O-18 values indicate relatively cooler paleotemperatures than those from other coexisting planktonic foraminiferal species. In addition, the delta C-13 values of G. eugubina are lighter than those of the other planktonic foraminifera. Such a relationship might suggest that G. eugubina lived in cooler, shallow intermediate water masses advected from higher latitudes during a latest Maastrichtian cooling episode. Better preservation of planktonic foraminifera, a progressive change in the delta C-13 of benthic foraminifera, and a brief positive excursion in delta C-13 of the carbonate fine fraction and of planktonic foraminifera within 20 to 30 cm below the K/T boundary all suggest important paleoceanographic changes that preceded the extinction event. A large population of aberrant G. eugubina and Eoglobigerina was observed in the earliest Danian. These forms are characterized by the development of secondary apertures, bullae, and abnormal final chambers. These abnormal morphotypes are considered to be ecophenotypic variants reflecting ecologic stress or instability in the earliest Cenozoic marine environment.
